#+STARTUP: content
* reformat zfs as ext4
** Step 1: Identify the Drive
First, we need to be 100% sure which drive is the external one.

#+begin_src sh
lsblk
#+end_src

Look for your drive based on its size. For this guide, I will assume your drive is identified as /dev/sdX.

Replace sdX with your actual identifier.

** Step 2: Clear the ZFS Metadata

ZFS is "sticky." Sometimes a simple format leaves ZFS signatures behind that confuse the OS.

We’ll wipe the filesystem signatures first.

Replace sdX with your actual identifier.

#+begin_src sh
sudo wipefs -a /dev/sdX
#+end_src

** Step 3: Create a New Partition Table

To ensure a clean slate, create a new GPT partition table:

#+begin_src sh
nix-shell -p parted
#+end_src

#+begin_src sh
doas parted /dev/sdX mklabel gpt
#+end_src

** Step 4: Create the Partition

Now, create a primary partition that spans the whole drive:

Replace sdX with your actual identifier.

#+begin_src sh
sudo parted -a optimal /dev/sdX mkpart primary ext4 0% 100%
#+end_src

** Step 5: Format to ext4

Now we layout the actual ext4 filesystem on the new partition (usually /dev/sdX1):

Replace sdX with your actual identifier.

#+begin_src sh
doas mkfs.ext4 /dev/sdX1
#+end_src

** Step 6: fix permissions

By default, a newly formatted ext4 drive is owned by root. To make it usable for your standard user account without using sudo every time, mount it and then change the ownership:

Mount it:

Replace sdX with your actual identifier.

#+begin_src sh
doas mount /dev/sdX1 /mnt
#+end_src

in this case the drive is mounted to

#+begin_example
/run/media/djwilcox/6653e0d1-af6c-4c85-bd2e-daf01073e183
#+end_example

Change owner:

#+begin_src sh
doas chown -R djwilcox:users /run/media/djwilcox/6653e0d1-af6c-4c85-bd2e-daf01073e183
#+end_src

** Give it a readable Label

Unmount it first

#+begin_src sh
doas umount /dev/sda1
#+end_src

Label it

#+begin_src sh
doas e2label /dev/sda1 videos
#+end_src
